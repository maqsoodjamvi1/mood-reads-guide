When the news cycle feels relentless and the people around you seem increasingly bitter, the temptation to mirror that cynicism is strong. It feels like a defensive shield. But lately, I’ve found that the best antidote isn’t toxic positivity or forced optimism; it’s reading about people who stay steady, capable, and kind in the face of messy, high-stakes situations.

Here are 10 books that feature characters who choose competence and decency as their primary way of navigating the world.

1. A Psalm for the Wild-Built by Becky Chambers

This is a “solarpunk” novella about a tea monk who travels from village to village listening to people’s problems. When they encounter a robot in the wilderness, the interaction isn’t one of conflict or fear, but of profound, thoughtful curiosity. It is the perfect read if you are burnt out by the idea that human progress must involve conflict.

2. The Guernsey Literary and Potato Peel Pie Society by Mary Ann Shaffer and Annie Barrows

Set in the aftermath of World War II, this story follows a writer corresponding with residents of a small island. The characters have every reason to be cynical after the German occupation, yet they focus on building community and protecting one another. It’s a masterclass in how small, consistent acts of kindness can rebuild a world.

3. Project Hail Mary by Andy Weir

If you prefer your competence with a side of physics, this is the book for you. The protagonist wakes up on a spaceship with no memory and a mission to save Earth. He doesn’t have time for ego or drama; he has to solve problems using math and scientific method. His willingness to collaborate—even with an unexpected partner—is a great reminder that working together is often the only way to solve big problems.

4. The House in the Cerulean Sea by TJ Klune

Linus Baker is a rigid, rule-following caseworker who oversees orphanages for magical children. He is the definition of a “gray man” until he is sent to a home where he discovers the beauty of being kind to those who are feared or misunderstood. It’s a reminder that empathy is an act of bravery, not weakness.

5. Remarkably Bright Creatures by Shelby Van Pelt

This story centers on Tova, a woman who works the night shift cleaning an aquarium, and Marcellus, a giant Pacific octopus who observes everything. Tova is impeccably competent at her job, even while grieving a deep loss. The way she keeps showing up for herself and her community is a quiet, powerful testament to the value of daily habits.

6. All Creatures Great and Small by James Herriot

These are memoirs from a country veterinarian in the 1930s. Herriot isn’t a superhero; he’s just a man trying to do his best for animals and their owners in rural Yorkshire. He deals with difficult farmers, bad weather, and exhaustion, but he never loses his patience or his sense of humor.

7. The No. 1 Ladies’ Detective Agency by Alexander McCall Smith

Precious Ramotswe opens a detective agency in Botswana. She isn’t interested in chasing villains; she’s interested in solving the small, human problems of her neighbors. Her approach to life—which relies on common sense, fairness, and deep observation—is incredibly refreshing.

8. Circe by Madeline Miller

While this is a mythological retelling, Circe is a character who carves out a life for herself through sheer, patient competence. Exiled and alone, she masters herbalism and magic through thousands of hours of study and failure. It’s a story about what you can achieve when you stop seeking external validation and focus on perfecting your own craft.

9. 84, Charing Cross Road by Helene Hanff

This is a true story told through letters between a writer in New York and a used-book dealer in London. Spanning twenty years, the correspondence creates a deep, platonic bond built on mutual respect and a shared love of literature. It highlights how generosity—in this case, sending food parcels to post-war Britain—can create a lifelong connection between strangers.

10. Anxious People by Fredrik Backman

Backman is a genius at writing about flawed, desperate people who are trying their best. This book revolves around a hostage situation at an apartment viewing. It sounds intense, but it’s actually a funny, warm exploration of how we are all dealing with things that others know nothing about. It challenges you to look at a “difficult” person and see the struggle beneath their behavior.

How to use this list

If you are feeling overwhelmed, don’t try to read all of these at once. Pick one that sounds like the specific type of comfort you need. If you want to feel intellectually energized, go with Project Hail Mary. If you want to feel a sense of quiet peace, start with A Psalm for the Wild-Built.

When you start reading, try to put your phone in another room for at least 30 minutes. The world of the book can only work its magic if you give it your undivided attention. Pay attention to how the characters handle their setbacks—do they immediately spiral, or do they look for the next logical step? Try to adopt that same step-by-step logic the next time you feel the weight of the world pressing down on you.

After you finish one of these, you might find yourself wanting to share the experience or explore other stories that highlight the better parts of human nature.
