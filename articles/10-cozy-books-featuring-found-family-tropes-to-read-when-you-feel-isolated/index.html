Loneliness can feel like a heavy, quiet room. Sometimes, the best way to move through that headspace isn’t to force yourself into a busy social calendar, but to find a soft place to land within the pages of a book.

“Found family” stories—where a group of misfits or strangers pull together to form a bond stronger than blood—are the perfect antidote for isolation. They offer a blueprint for belonging. When I’m feeling disconnected, I reach for these stories because they remind me that community isn’t always about who you were born to, but who you choose to keep in your orbit.

Here are 10 books that excel at the found family trope, curated for when you need a reminder that there is a place for you.

1. The House in the Cerulean Sea by TJ Klune

If you only read one book on this list, make it this one. It follows Linus Baker, a rigid caseworker who is sent to an orphanage for magical children to determine if they are dangerous. He expects to find monsters; he finds a family. It is unashamedly sweet and reads like a warm cup of tea on a rainy afternoon.

2. A Psalm for the Wild-Built by Becky Chambers

This is a short novella, perfect if your brain is too tired for a dense epic. A tea monk and a robot meet in the wilderness. They aren’t a traditional “family,” but their dialogue explores what it means to be human and to be lonely. It’s a quiet, philosophical hug of a book.

3. Legends & Lattes by Travis Baldree

An orc retires from her life of mercenary violence to open a coffee shop. Along the way, she collects a ragtag group of helpers—a carpenter, a succubus, and a rat—who become her staff and her chosen kin. If you like the idea of people building a business and a life together from scratch, this is the one.

4. The Long Way to a Small, Angry Planet by Becky Chambers

A classic for a reason. This follows the crew of the Wayfarer, a ship that builds wormholes. It is entirely character-driven. You watch these different species learn to navigate each other’s quirks and traumas. It feels like moving into a new apartment with roommates who eventually become your best friends.

5. The Very Secret Society of Irregular Witches by Sangu Mandanna

Mika Moon is a lonely witch who spends her life hiding her magic. She is hired to tutor three young witches in a remote house, and she ends up finding a permanent spot at their table. It’s romantic, whimsical, and focuses heavily on the idea that you deserve to be loved exactly as you are.

6. Six of Crows by Leigh Bardugo

If you prefer a little more grit with your found family, pick this up. It’s a heist story featuring six teenagers who are all outcasts in their own way. They don’t start out as friends; they start out as co-conspirators. Watching them learn to trust one another through a high-stakes job is incredibly satisfying.

7. The Starless Sea by Erin Morgenstern

This is for the reader who wants to disappear into a labyrinthine library. It’s atmospheric and dreamlike. The characters are brought together by a shared mystery, forming a bond that feels fated and deeply intimate. It is the perfect book to get lost in when you need to feel part of a secret, magical world.

8. Becky Chambers’ “Monk and Robot” series (again)

I’m doubling down because if you are feeling isolated, this series is the gold standard. The dynamic between the monk and the robot is the most patient, kind portrayal of friendship I’ve ever read. It strips away the pressure to be “productive” or “social” and just focuses on being present with another person.

9. In the Lives of Puppets by TJ Klune

Inspired by Pinocchio, this story features a human living in a forest with an android father and a small cleaning robot. When their home is threatened, they set off on a journey to find help. It’s quirky, funny, and deeply emotional. It proves that families can be built out of wires, metal, and good intentions.

10. The Gentleman’s Guide to Vice and Virtue by Mackenzi Lee

If you want something fast-paced and adventurous, this historical novel follows a group of young people on a Grand Tour of Europe. The chemistry between the leads is electric, and the way they shield each other from the world’s harshness is a masterclass in the trope.

How to use these books when you’re feeling lonely

When you feel the weight of isolation, don’t feel like you have to “fix” it by reading a self-help book. Sometimes, the most practical step is to treat yourself like you are one of the characters in these stories.

  • Create a ritual: Make a specific drink (tea, cocoa, or a fancy soda) only when you are reading these books. This anchors the reading experience to a comforting physical sensation.
  • Audiobooks for company: If the silence of your house is the problem, listen to these as audiobooks. Having a narrator talk in your ear while you do chores or sit on the porch makes the house feel lived-in and communal.
  • Take notes: When a character in these books has a breakthrough in their relationship with their “found family,” jot down what allowed them to open up. It can act as a gentle reminder of how to invite connection into your own life when you’re ready.

Books are a bridge. They can’t replace real-world human interaction, but they can lower your guard and help you remember how to be open to others. Once you’ve finished one of these, you might feel a little more equipped to reach out to a friend or simply sit in a public space, feeling less like an outsider and more like part of the human story.
